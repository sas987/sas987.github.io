Section 02 · Mechanics

02Motion & Newton's Laws

Three laws. Three real-world examples each. Done.
Newton's First Law — Law of Inertia
A body continues in its state of rest or uniform motion unless acted upon by an external force.
  • Example 1Passenger jerks forward when bus stops suddenly (inertia of motion).
  • Example 2Passenger jerks backward when bus starts (inertia of rest).
  • Example 3Dust falls off a carpet when beaten (carpet moves, dust at rest).
Newton's Second Law — F = ma
Rate of change of momentum is proportional to applied force. F = dp/dt = ma
  • Momentum (p)p = mv · vector quantity · SI: kg·m/s
  • ImpulseF × t = change in momentum (catching ball, airbags work on this)
  • Cricketer examplePulls hands back while catching → increases time → decreases force
Newton's Third Law — Action-Reaction
To every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. Act on different bodies.
  • Rocket propulsionGases pushed down → rocket pushed up
  • Recoil of gunBullet forward → gun backward (momentum conservation)
  • WalkingFoot pushes ground backward → ground pushes you forward
  • Boat & jumping manMan jumps forward → boat moves backward
Circular Motion — Centripetal vs Centrifugal
Centripetal force = real, acts toward center, F = mv²/r. Without it, body flies off tangentially.
Centrifugal force = pseudo (fictitious) force, felt only in rotating frame, acts outward.
  • Cream separatorUses centrifugal effect
  • Mud flies off cycle tyreTangential, due to inertia (not centrifugal!)
  • Banking of roadsProvides centripetal force on curves
A man jumps out of a moving train and falls. Why?
His feet stop with the ground, but the upper body retains forward momentum (Newton's First Law) — he topples forward.
Section 03 · Mechanics

03Work, Energy & Power

When is work zero? When does KE convert to PE? The asked questions.
Work — When Is It Zero?
W = F · d · cosθ. Work is zero in three cases:
  • F = 0No force applied
  • d = 0Body doesn't move (pushing a wall — favorite trick option)
  • θ = 90°Force ⊥ displacement (coolie carrying load on head walking horizontally; satellite in circular orbit)
Energy — Forms & Conservation
Law of Conservation of Energy: Energy can neither be created nor destroyed; only transformed.
ConversionDevice
Electrical → MechanicalElectric Motor
Mechanical → ElectricalDynamo / Generator
Electrical → HeatHeater, Iron, Geyser
Electrical → LightBulb, LED, Tubelight
Electrical → SoundLoudspeaker, Microphone (reverse)
Chemical → ElectricalBattery, Cell
Chemical → HeatCombustion, LPG burning
Solar → ElectricalSolar cell / Photovoltaic
Sound → ElectricalMicrophone
Light → ElectricalPhotocell
Kinetic vs Potential Energy
  • KE½mv² · energy of motion · scalar · always positive
  • PE (gravitational)mgh · stored energy due to position
  • Free-falling bodyPE decreases, KE increases, total mechanical energy constant
  • PendulumAt extreme: max PE, zero KE. At mean: max KE, zero PE.
Frequent Trap
Hydroelectric plants convert PE of water → KE → electrical, NOT directly PE to electrical. Two steps.
Section 04 · Mechanics

04Gravitation & Satellites

Where is g zero? Why doesn't a satellite fall? Pure prelims fodder.
Acceleration Due to Gravity (g)
Average g = 9.8 m/s² on Earth's surface. But not constant:
  • At PolesMaximum (≈9.83 m/s²) — Earth flattened at poles, smaller radius
  • At EquatorMinimum (≈9.78 m/s²) — bulge + rotation
  • With Altitude (height)Decreases (inverse square of distance from center)
  • With DepthDecreases linearly
  • At Earth's Centerg = 0 (mass pulls equally in all directions)
  • Weight on Moon1/6th of weight on Earth (g_moon ≈ 1.62 m/s²)
Escape Velocity
Minimum velocity to escape Earth's gravity permanently.
  • Earth's escape velocity11.2 km/s (≈40,320 km/h)
  • Moon's escape velocity≈2.4 km/s
  • Jupiter's escape velocity≈60 km/s (largest in solar system)
  • Independent ofMass of body, angle of projection
  • Depends onMass & radius of planet
Satellites
  • Geostationary satellite36,000 km altitude · period 24 hrs · equatorial plane · appears stationary · used for communication (INSAT, GSAT)
  • Polar (Sun-synchronous) sat800–900 km altitude · passes over poles · used for remote sensing (IRS, Cartosat)
  • Astronaut weightlessnessNOT because there's no gravity — because they are in free fall with the spacecraft
  • First satelliteSputnik-1 (USSR, 1957)
  • India's first satelliteAryabhata (1975, launched by USSR)
Why is escape velocity independent of body's mass?
In the derivation, mass of the body cancels out. v_esc = √(2GM/R) — depends only on the planet's M and R.
Section 05 · Mechanics

05Pressure, Buoyancy & Fluids

Why ice floats. Why pressure cooker cooks faster. Why iceberg sinks 8/9.
Pressure Basics
P = F/A. SI unit: Pascal (N/m²).
  • 1 atm (atmosphere)1.013 × 10⁵ Pa = 760 mm Hg = 76 cm Hg
  • Measured byBarometer (atmospheric); Manometer (gas/liquid); Sphygmomanometer (blood)
  • Hg used in barometerHigh density (so column is short ~76cm); doesn't wet glass
  • Sharp knife cuts betterSmall area → high pressure
  • Camel's wide feetLarge area → low pressure → won't sink in sand
Pressure Cooker & Altitude
  • Pressure cookerIncreased pressure → water boils at higher temp (~120°C) → food cooks faster
  • At high altitude (hills)Atmospheric pressure low → water boils at lower temp (~90°C at Shimla) → food half-cooked
  • Why airplane sealedLow pressure at altitude → cabin pressurized for breathing
  • Nose bleeds at altitudeOutside pressure low → internal blood pressure higher
Archimedes' Principle — The Floater
A body immersed in fluid experiences upthrust = weight of fluid displaced.
  • Floating conditionDensity of body ≤ Density of liquid
  • Ice floats on waterρ_ice = 0.92 g/cc < ρ_water = 1.0 g/cc
  • Iceberg1/10 above water, 9/10 below
  • Why ice melting doesn't raise water levelSubmerged volume = volume after melting
  • Ship floats despite ironHollow design → average density < water
  • HydrometerMeasures density of liquids using Archimedes
  • LactometerMeasures purity of milk (density-based)
Density Anomaly of Water
Water has maximum density at 4°C. Below 4°C, it expands (anomalous).
  • Why aquatic life survives in winterSurface freezes (less dense), water below 4°C stays liquid
  • Pipes burst in winterWater expands on freezing
  • Glass bottle of water cracksSame — ice has greater volume
Surface Tension & Capillarity
  • Surface tensionMinimizes surface area · why drops are spherical · why insects walk on water
  • Detergents/soapsReduce surface tension → better cleaning
  • CapillarityRise of liquid in narrow tubes · oil in lamp wick · ink rises in blotting paper · water in plants
  • Mercury in glass tubeFalls (depresses) — cohesion > adhesion
  • Water in glass tubeRises — adhesion > cohesion
  • ViscosityInternal friction in fluid · decreases with temperature (for liquids)
High-Frequency Trap
"A balloon filled with hydrogen rises because..." → Hydrogen is less dense than air → upthrust > weight. NOT "lighter" — density is the term.

Section 08 · Waves

08Sound & Acoustics

Audible range, SONAR, why sound is fastest in solids.
Sound — Fundamentals
Sound is a mechanical, longitudinal wave. It needs a medium — cannot travel through vacuum.
  • Speed in air (20°C)343 m/s
  • Speed in water≈1500 m/s
  • Speed in steel/iron≈5000 m/s · fastest in solids
  • General ruleSolid > Liquid > Gas (denser medium, faster)
  • Speed depends onIncreases with temperature, humidity · independent of pressure
  • Speed of light vs soundLight: 3 × 10⁸ m/s · Sound: 343 m/s · ~9 lakh times faster
  • Why lightning seen before thunderLight reaches first (much faster than sound)
Audible Range & Ultra/Infra Sound
  • Human audible range20 Hz to 20,000 Hz (20 kHz)
  • Infrasound (<20 Hz)Elephants, whales, rhinos · detected before earthquakes
  • Ultrasound (>20,000 Hz)Bats, dolphins, dogs · medical (sonography), industrial (defect testing), SONAR
  • Pitch / ShrillnessDepends on frequency · female voice higher pitch
  • LoudnessDepends on amplitude · measured in decibel (dB)
  • Quality / TimbreDistinguishes two sources at same pitch & loudness
Echo, Reverberation, SONAR
  • EchoReflected sound · requires min distance 17 m (time gap ≥ 0.1 s)
  • ReverberationMultiple reflections (in halls)
  • SONARSound Navigation And Ranging · uses ultrasound · measures sea depth, submarines
  • RADARRadio Detection & Ranging · uses radio waves (NOT sound)
  • Doppler effectApparent change in frequency due to relative motion · siren of approaching ambulance sounds higher pitch
The Vacuum Trap
"Sound cannot travel through?" → Vacuum (not "air"). Bell jar experiment: bell stops being audible as air is pumped out.

Plane, concave, convex — what is used WHERE.
Speed & Spectrum of Light
  • Speed of light in vacuum3 × 10⁸ m/s (exactly 299,792,458 m/s)
  • Maximum speed inVacuum (slows in denser medium)
  • Visible spectrumVIBGYOR · 400 nm (violet) to 700 nm (red)
  • Shortest wavelengthViolet (highest frequency)
  • Longest wavelengthRed (lowest frequency)
  • Beyond violetUV → X-rays → Gamma rays
  • Beyond redInfrared → Microwaves → Radio waves
Reflection & Mirrors
Laws: Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection. Both lie in same plane.
MirrorImageUse
PlaneVirtual, erect, same size, laterally invertedCommon mirror, periscope
Concave (converging)Real or virtual (depends on object position)Shaving, makeup, dentist, headlight of car, solar furnace, torch
Convex (diverging)Always virtual, erect, diminishedRear-view of vehicles (wider field of view), street light reflectors, security mirrors in shops

Convex for rear-view · Concave for headlight. Students always swap these. Memorize: "Outside diverging, inside converging."
Section 10 · Optics

10Refraction, Lenses & The Eye

Myopia vs hypermetropia — the most common BPSC eye question.
Refraction & Total Internal Reflection
Light bends when it enters a different medium. n = c/v (refractive index).
  • Denser to rarerBends away from normal
  • Rarer to denserBends towards normal
  • TIR conditionLight travels denser → rarer · angle > critical angle
  • Critical angle of glass≈42° (for air-glass)
  • Critical angle of water≈48.6°
  • Critical angle of diamond24° (smallest — that's why diamonds sparkle so much)
  • Applications of TIROptical fibre · mirage in deserts · sparkle of diamond · prismatic binoculars · endoscope
  • Pencil in glass of water appears bentRefraction
  • Pool appears shallowerRefraction (rarer medium air to denser water)
  • Stars twinkle, planets don'tStars are point sources — atmosphere refracts light variably. Planets have finite size → averages out.
Lenses — Convex & Concave
LensTypeApplication
ConvexConverging (+ve focal length)Magnifying glass · camera · projector · corrects hypermetropia
ConcaveDiverging (−ve focal length)Galilean telescope · corrects myopia
  • Power of lensP = 1/f (in metres) · unit: Dioptre (D)
  • Convex lens — powerPositive
  • Concave lens — powerNegative
The Human Eye
  • RetinaLight-sensitive screen at back of eye · contains rods (dim light, B&W) and cones (bright light, color)
  • Yellow spot (Fovea/Macula)Most sensitive · all cones · sharpest vision
  • Blind spotWhere optic nerve enters · NO rods or cones
  • IrisControls amount of light (changes pupil size)
  • AccommodationAbility of eye lens to change focal length (via ciliary muscles)
  • Persistence of vision1/16 second · basis of cinema/movies
  • Range of visionNear point (25 cm) to Far point (infinity)
Eye Defects & Corrections
DefectWhat HappensImage FormsCorrection
Myopia (short-sight)Can see near, not farBefore retinaConcave lens
Hypermetropia (long-sight)Can see far, not nearBehind retinaConvex lens
Presbyopia (old age)Both — loss of accommodation—Bifocal lens

Color blindnessCones malfunction—No optical cure (genetic, X-linked, more in males)
A person can read newspaper but cannot see distant board. What defect & cure?
Myopia (short-sightedness) · corrected by concave lens (diverging).
Section 11 · Optics

11Atmospheric Optics

Why sky is blue, sun red at sunset, rainbow — all of it.
Scattering of Light — Tyndall & Rayleigh
Rayleigh's law: Scattering ∝ 1/λ⁴ · Shorter wavelength scatters more.
  • Why sky appears blueBlue (short λ) scatters most by air molecules
  • Why sun appears red at sunrise/sunsetLong path through atmosphere → blue scattered away → only red reaches us
  • Why sun appears white at noonShortest path → all colors reach
  • Why danger signals are redRed scatters least → visible from farthest distance
  • Sky on Moon appears blackNo atmosphere → no scattering
  • Tyndall effectScattering by colloidal particles · why beam of sunlight visible in dusty room · seen in fog/smoke
Dispersion & Rainbow
  • DispersionSplitting of white light into 7 colors via prism · proved by Newton
  • RecombinationSecond prism inverted → recombines into white (Newton's experiment)
  • Most bentViolet (smallest λ)
  • Least bentRed (largest λ)
  • Rainbow processRefraction + Dispersion + Total Internal Reflection + Refraction in water droplets
  • Primary rainbow1 internal reflection · 42° angle · red outside, violet inside
  • Secondary rainbow2 internal reflections · 51° · order reversed (violet outside, red inside) · fainter
  • Sun position for rainbowBehind observer
Other Phenomena
  • MirageTIR in hot desert air · layers of air at different temps act as different media
  • LoomingOpposite of mirage (in cold polar regions) · distant objects appear lifted
  • Twinkling of starsAtmospheric refraction (continuously changing)
  • Apparent flattening of sun at sunsetRefraction in atmosphere
  • Sun visible before actual sunriseRefraction · ~2 minutes earlier · same after sunset

12Current Electricity

Ohm's law, series/parallel, AC vs DC — direct questions, no derivations.
Ohm's Law & Resistance
V = IR · Voltage = Current × Resistance.
  • Current (I)Rate of flow of charge · SI: Ampere (A) = C/s
  • Voltage / EMFSI: Volt (V) = J/C
  • Resistance (R)SI: Ohm (Ω) · R = ρL/A
  • Best conductorSilver > Copper > Gold > Aluminium
  • Why copper used in wiresGood conductivity + much cheaper than silver
  • Tungsten in bulbVery high melting point (~3400°C); high resistance
  • Nichrome in heaters/ironHigh resistance; doesn't oxidize easily at high temp
  • Resistance of insulatorsVery high (rubber, glass, dry wood)
  • Resistance of conductors with tempIncreases with temperature
  • Resistance of semiconductors with tempDecreases with temperature
  • SuperconductorZero resistance at very low temp (near 0 K)

Section 14 · Electromagnetism

14Magnetism & EMI

Oersted, Faraday, transformer — the trio.
Magnetism Basics
  • Magnetic field around currentDiscovered by Oersted (1820)
  • Right-hand thumb ruleThumb = current direction; fingers curl = magnetic field direction
  • Fleming's left-hand ruleFor force on current-carrying conductor in magnetic field (motor)
  • Fleming's right-hand ruleFor induced current (generator/dynamo)
  • Earth's magnetic north poleNear geographic SOUTH (that's why N of compass points geographic N)
  • DiamagneticWeakly repelled by magnet · bismuth, copper, water, gold
  • ParamagneticWeakly attracted · aluminium, platinum, oxygen
  • FerromagneticStrongly attracted · iron, cobalt, nickel
  • Magnet loses magnetism onHeating, hammering
  • Curie temperatureAbove this, ferromagnetic becomes paramagnetic (iron: 770°C)
Electromagnetic Induction (Faraday's Discovery)
When magnetic flux through a coil changes, an EMF is induced.
  • Discovered byMichael Faraday (1831)
  • Lenz's lawInduced current opposes the change causing it (energy conservation)
  • Generator (Dynamo)Mechanical → Electrical (uses EMI) · Fleming's right hand rule
  • Electric MotorElectrical → Mechanical · Fleming's left hand rule
  • TransformerChanges AC voltage · uses mutual induction · works only on AC
  • Step-up transformerN_s > N_p · ↑V, ↓I · used in power transmission
  • Step-down transformerN_s < N_p · ↓V, ↑I · used in domestic supply
  • Why AC for transmissionEasily stepped up to high voltage → less current → less I²R loss
  • Electromagnet usesSoft iron (loses magnetism quickly) · ferromagnetic, high permeability
  • Permanent magnet usesSteel (retains magnetism)
Why is the core of transformer laminated?
To reduce eddy current losses. Thin laminations break eddy current paths.

17Semiconductors & Lasers

Diode, transistor, LASER acronym — the modern essentials.
Semiconductors
Conductivity between conductor and insulator. Examples: Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge).
  • Intrinsic semiconductorPure (Si, Ge)
  • Extrinsic semiconductorDoped to increase conductivity
  • n-typeDoped with pentavalent (P, As) · majority carriers = electrons
  • p-typeDoped with trivalent (B, Al) · majority carriers = holes
  • Diode (p-n junction)Allows current in one direction · used in rectifiers

19BPSC Trap Patterns

The 20 trickiest physics questions BPSC consistently asks — pre-loaded answers.
First Indian to win Nobel Prize in Physics?
C.V. Raman (1930) — for Raman Effect. (NOT Tagore — he won in Literature.)
Father of Indian Space Programme?
Vikram Sarabhai · NOT APJ Abdul Kalam (he is "Missile Man").
Father of Indian Nuclear Programme?
Homi J. Bhabha.
Who discovered radioactivity?
Henri Becquerel (1896) · NOT Marie Curie (she discovered radium & polonium, 1898).
Speed of light in vacuum?
3 × 10⁸ m/s (precisely 299,792,458 m/s).
1 Horsepower equals how many watts?
746 W.
Audible range of human ear?
20 Hz to 20,000 Hz.
Mirror used in vehicle rear-view?
Convex (gives wider field of view; image always erect & diminished).
Mirror used in car/torch headlights?
Concave (parabolic — sends parallel beam).
Density of water is maximum at?
4°C (anomalous expansion below 4°C).
Value of g at Earth's centre?
Zero (mass pulls equally in all directions).
Eye defect corrected by concave lens?
Myopia (short-sightedness). Convex lens corrects Hypermetropia.
Why is sky blue?
Rayleigh scattering · shorter wavelengths (blue) scatter more.
Why is sun red at sunrise/sunset?
Long atmospheric path scatters away short wavelengths; only red survives.
Source of Sun's energy?
Nuclear fusion (Hydrogen → Helium), NOT fission.
Element used in atomic bomb?
Uranium-235 or Plutonium-239.
Moderator used in nuclear reactor?
Heavy water (D₂O) or graphite — slows down neutrons.
Best conductor of electricity?
Silver · followed by Copper. (Copper is used in wires because it's cheaper.)
Why does a swimming pool appear shallower than it is?
Refraction of light from denser water to rarer air.
Why doesn't sound travel in vacuum?
Sound is a mechanical wave — needs a medium for propagation.
