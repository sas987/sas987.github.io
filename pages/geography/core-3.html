Major Cities — Geographical Profile

Top 6 cities by population & importance — every fact testable
★ Largest City · State Capital
Patna
Pataliputra · Kusumpur · Azimabad — historical capital of Magadha, Maurya, Gupta empires
Patna is on the south bank of Ganga at its confluence with three other rivers: Ganga + Son + Gandak + Punpun. This 4-river confluence made it strategically vital across history.

Ancient names: Pataliputra (capital of Mauryan + Gupta empires); Kusumpur, Azimabad (Mughal era — named after Azim-us-Shan, Aurangzeb's grandson).

Modern Patna spans approximately 15 km along the Ganga. Mahatma Gandhi Setu (5.575 km) was once Asia's longest river bridge connecting Patna to Hajipur.
Population (2011)~58 lakh (largest in Bihar)
Rank in India~12th largest city
AirportJay Prakash Narayan Int'l
NotablePatna Sahib Gurudwara · Bihar Museum · Golghar
★ 2nd Largest · Religious Tourism Capital
Gaya
Buddha's enlightenment site · Hindu pitra-moksha center
Gaya sits on the Phalgu river (Niranjana + Mohane confluence). It's the only Bihar city with both Buddhist and Hindu world-pilgrimage status.

Bodh Gaya (~13 km from Gaya city) — Mahabodhi Temple where Buddha attained enlightenment; UNESCO World Heritage Site (2002).

Vishnupad Temple in Gaya city — Hindus from across the world come to perform Pind Daan (ancestral rites).

Gaya has Bihar's highest summer temperature (45–48°C), and the Tropic of Cancer passes through Gaya district.
AirportGaya International (Buddhist circuit)

Bhagalpur
Silk City · Vikramshila · Tropic of Cancer
Bhagalpur is on the south bank of the Ganga in eastern Bihar. The Tropic of Cancer (23.5°N) passes through Bhagalpur district.

Silk industry: 200+ years old; produces Tussar silk (GI tagged 2013). The handloom industry employs lakhs.

Vikramshila University ruins nearby — ancient Buddhist learning center (8th–12th century), competitor to Nalanda.

Vikramshila Gangetic Dolphin Sanctuary — 60 km Ganga stretch protecting India's National Aquatic Animal.

Section 14

Natural Disaster Zones

Floods, droughts, earthquakes — Bihar is multi-hazard vulnerable
Flood-Prone Zones — North Bihar

Bihar accounts for ~17% of India's flood-affected area and ~22% of India's flood-affected population — making it India's most flood-vulnerable state.

⚠ Flood Statistics

28 of 38 districts in Bihar are officially flood-prone. The "flood corridor" runs across 76,000 sq km of Bihar — almost the entire North Bihar plus parts of South Bihar near Ganga. This is the highest flood-vulnerable area of any Indian state.

Drought-Prone Zones — South Bihar

While North Bihar floods, South Bihar simultaneously experiences drought in the same season — the famous "flood-drought paradox" of Bihar.

Drought-Prone DistrictsReason
Aurangabad, Gaya, Nawada, Jehanabad, ArwalMagadh region — low rainfall, no perennial rivers, hot summers
Kaimur, Rohtas (parts)Lowest rainfall zone in Bihar (~900–1000mm); plateau region

Earthquake (Seismic) Zones

Bihar lies in the Himalayan seismic belt — making North Bihar especially earthquake-vulnerable. The 1934 Bihar-Nepal earthquake (8.0 magnitude) killed ~10,000+ people and remains one of India's worst earthquakes.

⚠ Zone V — Very High Risk
Districts: Most of North Bihar near Nepal:
West Champaran, East Champaran, Sheohar, Sitamarhi, Madhubani, Supaul, Araria, Kishanganj.

Significance: Highest seismic risk in India along with NE states, J&K. The 1934 Great Bihar-Nepal Earthquake (M 8.0) struck this belt.
⚠ Zone IV — High Risk
Districts: Most of remaining Bihar including Patna, Muzaffarpur, Darbhanga, Saran, Vaishali, Begusarai, Bhagalpur, Munger.

Significance: Risk lower than Zone V but still significant — buildings need earthquake-resistant design.

  • 1934 Bihar-Nepal Earthquake — Magnitude 8.0; epicenter in Nepal; killed 10,000–12,000 people; destroyed Muzaffarpur, Darbhanga, Sitamarhi
  • 1988 Bihar-Nepal Earthquake — Magnitude 6.8; killed ~1,000 people
  • 2015 Nepal Earthquake — Magnitude 7.8; aftershocks felt across North Bihar; affected Sitamarhi, Madhubani, Supaul
