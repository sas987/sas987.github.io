★ How BPSC Tests Indian Geography (Last 30 Years)

Analysis of 39th–70th BPSC papers shows Indian Geography is the highest-weightage geography section (10–12 questions per paper). Confirmed BPSC pattern:

  • Direct factual: "Which is the highest peak in India?", "Tropic of Cancer passes through which states?"
  • River-region pairs: "Indravati & Sabari are tributaries of which river?" (CDPO 2018: Godavari); "Chandra-Bhaga in which region?" (66th BPSC: Lahaul)
  • Climate factual: "Mango Shower is related to?" (43rd BPSC); "Maximum rainfall from which monsoon?" (52nd BPSC)
  • Comparative: "Amritsar-Shimla climate difference reason?" (53rd-55th BPSC: altitude)
  • NO complex multi-statement format like UPSC — BPSC keeps it factual

"Indian Geography is BPSC's highest-weightage geography section. Master it completely and you lock 10–12 marks. Combined with Bihar (50) and World Geography (12), your geography total crosses 70 marks alone."

Section 04

The Himalayas — Overview

★ HIGHEST-WEIGHTAGE TOPIC — every BPSC paper has 1-2 Himalayan questions
TypeYoung fold mountains (Tertiary period); still rising ~5mm/year
FormationCaused by collision of Indian Plate with Eurasian Plate (started ~50 million years ago)
Length~2,400 km — Indus gorge (west, Pakistan) to Brahmaputra bend (east, Arunachal)
Width150-400 km (narrowest in east, widest in west — Kashmir region)
CountriesIndia, Pakistan, Nepal, Bhutan, China (Tibet)
Indian states coveredJ&K, Ladakh (UT), HP, Uttarakhand, Sikkim, West Bengal (Darjeeling), Arunachal Pradesh
Highest peakMt. Everest (8,849 m) — Nepal-China; India's: Kangchenjunga (8,586 m)
Indo-Burmese rangeEastern extension of Himalayas — through Nagaland, Manipur, Mizoram
Significance of Himalayas to India
  • Climatic barrier: Blocks cold Siberian winds; intercepts SW monsoon → causes heavy rainfall
  • Source of rivers: Ganga, Brahmaputra, Indus and their tributaries originate here
  • Glaciers: Major glaciers like Siachen, Gangotri, Yamunotri — perennial water source
  • Biodiversity: Eastern Himalayas = one of 4 global biodiversity hotspots in India
  • Forests: Tropical evergreen → temperate → alpine zones
  • Mineral resources: Petroleum, coal, copper, lead, zinc
  • Tourism: Hill stations, religious pilgrimages, adventure tourism
  • Strategic: Forms India's natural northern frontier
"The Himalayas are young fold mountains formed by collision of which two plates?" → Indian Plate and Eurasian Plate. Tested in nearly every BPSC paper. Also asked: "The Himalayas rise approximately ___ mm per year" → ~5mm.
Section 05

Himalayan Divisions — Longitudinal & Regional

3 longitudinal ranges + 4 regional divisions — both formats tested
Longitudinal Divisions (North to South) — 3 Parallel Ranges
Greater Himalayas (Himadri) ★ HIGHEST Northernmost · Permanent snow
Average height: ~6,100 m. Width: ~25 km.
Features: Contains all major Himalayan peaks (Everest, K2, Kangchenjunga, Nanda Devi, Annapurna); permanently snow-covered; source of all major Himalayan rivers and glaciers.
Composition: Igneous + metamorphic rocks (granite, gneiss).
Major glaciers: Siachen, Gangotri, Yamunotri, Pindari, Zemu, Milam.
Asymmetry: Steeper on south side (Indian side); gentler northward (Tibetan plateau).
Lesser / Middle Himalayas (Himachal) MID-RANGE Middle range · Hill stations
Average height: 3,700–4,500 m. Width: ~50 km.
Famous ranges: Pir Panjal (longest of Lesser Himalayas), Dhauladhar, Mussoorie, Mahabharat, Nag Tibba.
Famous valleys: Kashmir Valley, Kangra Valley, Kullu Valley.
Famous hill stations: Shimla, Mussoorie, Nainital, Darjeeling, Ranikhet, Almora, Chail — most located in Lesser Himalayas.
Composition: Compressed sedimentary + metamorphic rocks.
Shivalik / Outer Himalayas YOUNGEST Southernmost · Lowest
Average height: 900–1,200 m. Width: ~50 km.
Significance: Youngest of the 3 ranges; composed of unconsolidated sediments brought down by Himalayan rivers.
Features: Duns — longitudinal valleys between Shivalik and Lesser Himalayas (e.g., Dehradun, Kotli Dun, Patli Dun).
Risk: Highly landslide-prone due to soft sedimentary composition + steep slopes.
"Pir Panjal is part of which Himalayan division?" → Lesser Himalayas. "Which is the youngest Himalayan range?" → Shivalik. "The Duns are located between?" → Shivalik and Lesser Himalayas. All confirmed BPSC patterns.

Section 08

The Northern Plains (Indo-Gangetic Plain)

India's most fertile region — Bihar is part of this
Length~2,400 km (west to east)
Width240-320 km (narrowest in east — Bihar; widest in west — Punjab)
Area~7 lakh sq km
FormationAlluvium deposited by Indus, Ganga, Brahmaputra and their tributaries
StatesPunjab, Haryana, Delhi, UP, Bihar, West Bengal (+ parts of Rajasthan, Jharkhand, Assam)
4 Divisions of Northern Plains
Bhabar FOOTHILLS Just south of Shivalik
Width: 8-16 km belt of boulders, gravels, pebbles at foothills of Shivalik.
Special feature: Rivers disappear underground here (porous coarse sediments); reappear in Terai.
Not suitable for agriculture due to coarse soil.
Terai MARSHY South of Bhabar · wetlands
Width: 15-30 km. Marshy + swampy due to re-emergence of rivers from underground (Bhabar).
Forest cover: Dense forests historically; Tharu tribe lives here (especially in West Champaran, Bihar; UP; Nepal Terai).
Wildlife: Dudhwa Tiger Reserve (UP); Jim Corbett NP (Uttarakhand); Valmiki Tiger Reserve (Bihar Champaran).
Bhangar (Older Alluvium) OLDER · UPLAND Higher ground · less fertile
Older alluvium on higher terraces; contains kankar (calcareous concretions).
Less fertile than Khadar; rarely flooded.
Khadar (Newer Alluvium) NEWER · FERTILE Annual floodplains · most fertile
Newer alluvium deposited annually by river floods.
Most fertile soil in India; intensive agriculture; densely populated (Bihar's Khadar areas are highest density in India).
"Bhabar belt is characterized by?" → Pebbles, gravel; rivers disappear underground. "Khadar vs Bhangar — which is newer and more fertile?" → Khadar (newer + more fertile). These are repeated BPSC concepts.

Section 10

Western & Eastern Ghats

Two parallel ranges flanking the Deccan
Western Ghats (Sahyadri) ★ UNESCO HERITAGE ~1,600 km · 6 states
Length: ~1,600 km from Tapi (Gujarat-Maharashtra border) to Kanyakumari.
States: Gujarat, Maharashtra, Goa, Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu.
Average height: 900-1,600 m; higher in south.
Composition: Continuous range; only broken by passes.
Highest peak: Anamudi (2,695 m) in Kerala — highest peak in South India.
Other peaks: Doddabetta (Nilgiris, 2,637 m); Mahabaleshwar (1,438 m).
Climate: Heavy rainfall on western side (windward); rain shadow on east.
UNESCO Heritage: Declared in 2012 — biodiversity hotspot; ~5,000 species of flowering plants.
Major passes: Thal Ghat, Bhor Ghat, Pal Ghat (3 famous passes connecting east-west).
Eastern Ghats DISCONTINUOUS ~1,400 km · 5 states
Length: ~1,400 km from Mahanadi delta (Odisha) to Vaigai river (Tamil Nadu).
States: Odisha, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka.
Average height: 600-900 m; much lower than Western Ghats.
Composition: Discontinuous (broken by river valleys); rivers Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, Kaveri cut through.
Highest peak: Arma Konda (1,680 m) in Andhra Pradesh's Visakhapatnam district. Earlier widely called "Jindhagada Peak" or "Mahendragiri".
Other peaks: Mahendragiri (Odisha, 1,501 m).
Where Western + Eastern Ghats meet: Nilgiri Hills (in TN-Karnataka-Kerala junction); Doddabetta is the highest peak.

Nilgiri Hills + Junction

The Nilgiri Hills are where the Western and Eastern Ghats meet — in the Tamil Nadu-Karnataka-Kerala junction. Doddabetta (2,637 m) is the highest peak of Nilgiris. Famous tea-growing region. Nilgiri Tahr is the state animal of Tamil Nadu and endemic to these hills.

"Anamudi is the highest peak of?" → Western Ghats (and South India). "Where do Western and Eastern Ghats meet?" → Nilgiri Hills. "Highest peak in Eastern Ghats?" → Arma Konda (Andhra Pradesh). Confirmed BPSC patterns.

Section 11

Coastal Plains of India

Western + Eastern coastal plains — distinct features
Western Coastal Plain NARROW ~1,500 km · narrow strip
Length: ~1,500 km from Gulf of Khambhat to Kanyakumari.
Width: 50-100 km (narrow due to Western Ghats).
States: Gujarat, Maharashtra, Goa, Karnataka, Kerala.
Sub-divisions:
  • • Konkan Coast (Mumbai-Goa) — Maharashtra-Goa
  • • Kanara Coast (Goa-Mangalore) — Karnataka
  • • Malabar Coast (Mangalore-Kanyakumari) — Kerala
Special features: Backwaters (Kerala); estuaries — Narmada-Tapi (NOT deltas); ports — Mumbai, JNPT, Mormugao, New Mangalore, Kochi.
Rivers flowing west: Narmada, Tapi, Sabarmati, Mahi — all form estuaries (not deltas).
Eastern Coastal Plain WIDE · DELTAIC ~1,500 km · wide
Length: ~1,500 km from Ganga delta (West Bengal) to Kanyakumari.
Width: 100-200 km (wider than west).
States: West Bengal, Odisha, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu.
Sub-divisions:
  • • Utkal Coast — Odisha; includes Chilika Lake
  • • Andhra Coast — Andhra Pradesh; includes Pulicat Lake
  • • Coromandel Coast — Tamil Nadu coast
Special features: Multiple river deltas (Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, Kaveri) — fertile; ports — Kolkata, Paradip, Visakhapatnam, Chennai, Tuticorin.
Climate: Coromandel coast receives rain from Northeast monsoon (Oct-Dec) — unique in India.
"Coromandel coast receives heavy rainfall in which months?" → October-November from Northeast monsoon (52nd BPSC 2008 confirmed pattern). Coromandel is the only major coast that gets winter monsoon. Memorize this exception.

Islands of India

Two island UTs + minor islands
Andaman & Nicobar Islands BAY OF BENGAL 572 islands · only ~38 inhabited
Location: Bay of Bengal; closer to Myanmar/Indonesia than mainland India.
Total islands: 572 (~38 inhabited).
Capital: Port Blair (in South Andaman).
Origin: Volcanic + Tectonic origin; extension of Arakan Yoma (Myanmar) mountains.
Highest peak: Saddle Peak (732 m) in North Andaman.
Active volcano: Barren Island — India's only active volcano (last erupted 2017).
Dormant volcano: Narcondam Island.
Indira Point — southernmost tip of India; on Great Nicobar Island; submerged after 2004 tsunami.
10° Channel — separates Andaman from Nicobar groups.
Tribes: Sentinelese (uncontacted), Jarwa, Onge, Shompen, Great Andamanese (PVTGs).
Lakshadweep Islands ARABIAN SEA 36 islands · only ~10 inhabited
Location: Arabian Sea; off Kerala coast.
Total islands: 36 (only ~10 inhabited).
Capital: Kavaratti.
Origin: Coral origin — atolls and reefs.
India's smallest UT by area (~32 sq km).
Major islands: Kavaratti, Agatti (has airport), Minicoy (southernmost), Bangaram, Kalpeni, Kadmat.
8° Channel — separates Lakshadweep from Maldives (south of Minicoy).
9° Channel — separates Lakshadweep main group from Minicoy.
"India's only active volcano is on which island?" → Barren Island (in Andaman). "Indira Point is on which island?" → Great Nicobar (southernmost point of India). "Lakshadweep islands are made of?" → Coral. "10° Channel separates?" → Andaman and Nicobar. All confirmed BPSC patterns.
