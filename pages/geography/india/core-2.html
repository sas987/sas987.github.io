67th BPSC 2022 asked exactly this comparison. Key facts memorise: Himalayan rivers are perennial; Peninsular rivers are seasonal/rain-fed. Himalayan rivers form deltas; west-flowing peninsular rivers form estuaries. Common 67th BPSC option: "Slope of Himalayan river is steeper than peninsular" — TRUE in upper course; in plains it's gentler.

Section 02

The Indus River System

★ 68th BPSC 2022 asked: Indus meets Kabul River at Attock
Indus (Sindhu) ★ HISTORIC Westernmost Himalayan system · 3,180 km
Source: Glacier near Mount Kailash (Mansarovar region, Tibet) at ~4,164 m altitude.
Length: ~3,180 km total; only ~1,114 km flows in India.
Path: Tibet → enters India via Ladakh → flows through J&K → enters Pakistan → joins Arabian Sea (Karachi).
At Attock (Pakistan): Joined by Kabul River (Afghanistan tributary) — frequently asked.
Tributaries (5 famous rivers of Punjab): Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Beas, Sutlej — these 5 meet to form Panjnad, which joins Indus.
Significance: Indus Valley Civilization developed here; "Sindh" / "Hindu" / "India" all derive from "Sindhu".
Indus Water Treaty (1960): India-Pakistan; brokered by World Bank. India gets eastern rivers (Ravi, Beas, Sutlej); Pakistan gets western rivers (Indus, Jhelum, Chenab).
OriginMansarovar (Tibet, China)
MouthArabian Sea (Karachi, Pakistan)
India length1,114 km (mainly Ladakh)
StatesLadakh, J&K (Kashmir)
68th BPSC 2022 confirmed: "At the end of its mountainous journey at Attock, the Indus river joins which river of Afghanistan?" → Kabul River. This exact question was asked. Remember this fact.

Ganga splits in West Bengal: Bhagirathi-Hooghly (in W. Bengal, India) + Padma (in Bangladesh).

The Brahmaputra River System

India's most voluminous river — and the famous "U-turn"
Brahmaputra ★ HIGH VOLUME "U-turn river" · 2,900 km
Source: Chemayungdung Glacier near Mt. Kailash (Tibet) — also fed from Mansarovar area.
Names along course:
  • • Tsangpo in Tibet (China)
  • • Siang / Dihang in Arunachal Pradesh (India entry)
  • • Brahmaputra in Assam
  • • Jamuna in Bangladesh
  • • Padma (after meeting Ganga in Bangladesh)
  • • Meghna (final stretch before Bay of Bengal)
The Famous "U-Turn": Near Namcha Barwa (Tibet), Tsangpo makes a sharp U-turn → enters India in Arunachal. UPSC 2011 explanation: Due to "syntaxial bending of geologically young Himalayas".
Total length: ~2,900 km; in India: ~916 km.
Width: Up to 10 km wide in Assam plains — broadest river in India.
Important river island: Majuli (Assam) — world's largest river island; in Brahmaputra.
Major tributaries (Brahmaputra): Subansiri, Manas, Dibang, Lohit, Kameng (north bank); Burhi Dihing, Disang (south bank); Teesta joins in Bangladesh.
Flood characteristic: Causes major Assam floods annually.

MouthBay of Bengal (via Bangladesh)

"The Brahmaputra is known as ___ in Tibet" → Tsangpo. "___ in Arunachal Pradesh" → Siang/Dihang. "___ in Bangladesh" → Jamuna. Match-the-pair questions on Brahmaputra's names appear in BPSC.
Teesta River

Important Himalayan river — tributary of Brahmaputra. Originates in Sikkim Himalayas; flows through Sikkim → West Bengal → enters Bangladesh → joins Jamuna (Brahmaputra). Major rivers Teesta passes near: Gangtok, Siliguri. Was historically a tributary of Ganga (changed course in 1787 earthquake).

East-Flowing Peninsular Rivers

Drain to Bay of Bengal; all form deltas — Godavari, Krishna, Kaveri, Mahanadi
Godavari "DAKSHIN GANGA" India's 2nd longest river · 1,465 km
Source: Triambakeshwar, Nashik (Maharashtra).
Length: 1,465 km — longest river entirely in peninsular India, and longest river entirely within India (Ganga is longer but exits via Bangladesh).
States: Maharashtra, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh; small portion in Madhya Pradesh, Karnataka, Odisha.

Notable cities: Nashik, Rajahmundry.
Cyclone-prone delta: Major impact zone for Bay of Bengal cyclones.

West-Flowing Peninsular Rivers

Drain to Arabian Sea; form estuaries (not deltas) — Narmada, Tapi, Mahi, Sabarmati, Luni
Narmada "LIFELINE OF MP & GUJARAT" 1,312 km · flows through rift valley
Source: Amarkantak Plateau (MP) at ~1,057 m elevation.
Length: 1,312 km — 5th longest river in India; longest west-flowing.
States: Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra (small), Gujarat.
Mouth: Gulf of Khambhat (Arabian Sea) — forms ESTUARY, not delta.
Why estuary? Narmada flows through a rift valley between Vindhyas and Satpuras → steep banks prevent delta formation.
Tributaries: Hiran, Burhner, Banjar, Tawa.
Special: Sardar Sarovar Dam (one of largest dams in India); important navigation for Gujarat.
Sacred: Parikrama (circumambulation) tradition; Marble Rocks at Bhedaghat.
Tapi (Tapti) 2ND LONGEST WEST 724 km
Source: Multai (Satpura Range, MP).
States: Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Gujarat.
Mouth: Gulf of Khambhat (Arabian Sea); flows past Surat.
Like Narmada: Flows through rift valley (Satpura - Maharashtra plateau); forms estuary.
Tributaries: Purna, Girna, Panjhra.
Mahi GUJARAT 580 km
Source: Vindhyan Range (MP).
States: MP, Rajasthan, Gujarat.
Mouth: Gulf of Khambhat.
Crosses Tropic of Cancer TWICE — unique feature (no other major Indian river does this).
Sabarmati GANDHI'S RIVER 371 km
Source: Aravalli range (Rajasthan).
Mouth: Gulf of Khambhat.
Famous for: Sabarmati Ashram in Ahmedabad — Gandhi's residence (1917-1930); Dandi March started from here.
Sabarmati Riverfront: Ahmedabad's urban riverfront development.
Luni ★ DESERT RIVER Rajasthan's only major river
Source: Aravalli range (Pushkar lake area, Rajasthan).
States: Rajasthan, Gujarat.
End point: Lost in the marshy land of Rann of Kutch — UPSC 2010, BPSC repeated question.
Special: Becomes saline as it flows through Thar Desert; only major river of Rajasthan desert region.
"Luni river ends in?" → Marshy land of Rann of Kutch (UPSC 2010; repeated by BPSC). NOT Gulf of Khambhat. Many candidates wrongly choose Gulf of Khambhat — that's the TRAP.
Why Peninsular West-Flowing Rivers Don't Form Deltas

The west-flowing peninsular rivers (Narmada, Tapi) flow through rift valleys with steep gradients → reach Arabian Sea fast → carry sediment further into sea → form estuaries, not deltas. Compare with east-flowing rivers (Godavari, Krishna, Kaveri) that flow gently across plateau → deposit sediments → form deltas.

"Narmada and Tapi form estuaries, not deltas" — why? Answer: Because they flow through rift valleys with steep gradients. Asked in BPSC repeatedly.
PYQ Pattern — BPSC
"Narmada and Tapi are the only major Indian rivers that flow through ___?"
Rift valleys — between Vindhyas-Satpuras (Narmada) and Satpura-Maharashtra plateau (Tapi)
PYQ Pattern — BPSC
"Which Indian river crosses the Tropic of Cancer twice?"
Mahi River — unique feature

4 Seasons of India (IMD Classification)
SeasonMonthsCharacteristics
Cold Weather Season (Winter)December – FebruaryCold north; mild south; northeast monsoon rain on Coromandel; Western Disturbances bring rain to NW

Factors Affecting Indian Climate
  • Latitude: Tropic of Cancer passes through middle → south tropical, north sub-tropical
  • Himalayas: Block Siberian cold winds; cause orographic rainfall
  • Distance from sea: Coastal regions = moderate; inland = extreme
  • Altitude: Higher = colder (Shimla vs Amritsar despite same latitude — BPSC 53rd-55th 2011)
  • Pressure & Winds: Monsoon pattern caused by differential heating
  • Western Disturbances: From Mediterranean → bring winter rain to NW India
  • El Niño: Weakens monsoon
  • La Niña: Strengthens monsoon
  • Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD): Positive IOD = good monsoon; Negative = poor

Section 11

Southwest Monsoon (Advancing Monsoon)

★ Brings ~75% of India's annual rainfall — single highest BPSC frequency topic
★ SW Monsoon — How It Works

In summer, the Tibetan Plateau heats up dramatically → creates low pressure over North India. Simultaneously, the Indian Ocean (south of equator) is relatively cool → high pressure. Air rushes from south (high P) to north (low P), crossing the equator. As trade winds (SE) cross equator, Coriolis force deflects them → become SW winds when they enter India. These winds pick up moisture from Indian Ocean → bring monsoon to India.

This is the differential heating of land & sea mechanism — confirmed BPSC answer.

2 Branches of SW Monsoon
Arabian Sea Branch WESTERN BRANCH
Path: Strikes Western Ghats → causes heavy rainfall on windward side (~2,500-5,000 mm) → rain shadow on Deccan Plateau (~600 mm).
Sub-branches:
  • • Branch 1: Western Ghats → causes Kerala-Karnataka-Goa-Maharashtra rainfall
  • • Branch 2: Northern branch → enters from Mumbai → reaches Saurashtra-Kutch → Rajasthan
  • • Branch 3: Sub-Himalayan branch → joins Bay of Bengal branch in Punjab
First monsoon arrival in India: Kerala on June 1 (Arabian Sea branch).
Bay of Bengal Branch EASTERN BRANCH
Path: Strikes NE India → brings heavy rainfall to NE (Cherrapunji-Mawsynram zone in Meghalaya gets world's highest rainfall) → curves westward toward Indo-Gangetic plain.
Hits Bihar: From east, around mid-June.
Bihar's monsoon onset: Mid-June (mid-June; about 15-20 days after Kerala).
Both branches merge: Over UP/Punjab — give combined rainfall.
Why Mawsynram has highest rainfall? Funnel shape of Khasi Hills + Bay of Bengal Branch hitting it.

Section 12

NE Monsoon & Monsoon Retreat

★ Special — Coromandel coast gets rain only from NE monsoon
★ How NE Monsoon Works

By October, Tibetan Plateau cools → high pressure builds over North India → wind direction reverses → blows from NE (land) to SW (sea). As NE winds cross Bay of Bengal, they pick up moisture → bring rainfall to Tamil Nadu coast (Coromandel).

NE monsoon contributes only ~25% of TN's annual rainfall — but it's the only major coast in India that gets winter monsoon rain.

Why Western Disturbances Matter for India
  • Originate in Mediterranean Sea region; travel eastward as mid-latitude storms
  • Picked up by Subtropical Jet Stream → reach NW India
  • Bring winter rainfall to Punjab, HP, Kashmir, parts of UP and Bihar
  • Beneficial for Rabi crops (wheat, barley, gram, mustard) — without this rain, wheat yield would crash
  • Bring snowfall to Himalayan states; replenish glaciers
  • Can cause flash floods in Kashmir, HP if too intense

★ India's Rainfall Records
  • Wettest place: Mawsynram, Meghalaya (~11,872 mm)
  • Wettest state: Meghalaya (or Mizoram, depending on year)
  • Driest state: Rajasthan (~150 mm)
  • India's annual average rainfall: ~125 cm (1250 mm)
  • Why Mawsynram-Cherrapunji are wettest: Khasi Hills form a funnel that traps Bay of Bengal monsoon
  • Rain shadow: Deccan Plateau is in rain shadow of Western Ghats — eastern slopes get less rain
