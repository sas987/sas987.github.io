Section 02

Physical Divisions of Bihar

Post-2000 Bihar has 3 physiographic units — questions test terrain features, not just names

After Jharkhand's separation (which took most of the Chota Nagpur Plateau), present-day Bihar has three main physiographic divisions. The Ganga river is the defining spine.

① North Bihar Plains (Tarai)
  • Between Nepal Himalayas and Ganga river
  • Sub-zones: Bhabar → Tarai → Alluvial Plains
  • Bhabar: narrow porous gravel zone at foothills (West Champaran)
  • Tarai: marshy dense forest (Valmiki Tiger Reserve)
  • Braided rivers, frequent floods (Kosi, Gandak, Bagmati)
  • Most fertile zone; "granary" of Bihar
② South Bihar Plains
  • Between Ganga and Jharkhand border
  • Older, more stable alluvial land
  • Southern fringe: Rajgir Hills, Kaimur Plateau
  • Less flood-prone than North Bihar
  • Rivers: Son, Punpun, Phalgu, Karmnasa
  • More drought-prone (less rainfall)
③ Ganga Plains (The Dividing Corridor)
  • The Ganga river valley itself — separates North and South Bihar plains
  • Width ~6–8 km near Patna; floodplain deposits (Khadar — newer alluvium)
  • Ganga enters Bihar at Chausa, Buxar (from UP) and exits near Rajmahal into West Bengal
Alluvial Deposits: Khadar vs Bhangar
FeatureKhadar (Newer Alluvium)Bhangar (Older Alluvium)
LocationNear river banks, floodplainsElevated areas, away from rivers
FertilityMore fertile, renewed each floodLess fertile, more leached
Special featureFiner particles, loamyContains Kankars (calcium nodules)
Bihar beltGanga corridor, North Bihar riversParts of South Bihar, Kaimur
High-Frequency BPSC Land-Type Concepts
  • Diara land: Alluvial islands/land in middle of rivers (esp. Ganga). Fertile but flood-prone. Districts: Patna, Bhojpur, Saran, Vaishali.
  • Chaur: Shallow seasonal lakes/depressions in North Bihar — poor drainage. Kanwar Lake (Begusarai) is a famous chaur (oxbow type). Makhana grows in chaurs.
  • Tal: Lower waterlogged depressions in South Bihar. Gaya, Nalanda districts.
  • Terai: Marshy zone at base of Himalayas — West Champaran; Valmiki National Park.

⚠ Critical Kosi Facts — Appear Every Few Papers

Kosi Project / Bhimnagar Barrage (1959): Indo-Nepal joint project at Supaul district border. Controls floods; provides irrigation to North Bihar and Nepal.

2008 Kosi Disaster: Kosi breached the embankment at Kusaha (Nepal) and suddenly shifted course ~100 km eastward. Affected 40+ lakh people in Bihar — one of independent India's worst flood disasters.

Why "Sorrow"? Highest silt load of all Himalayan rivers → raises its own bed → overflows embankments → catastrophic annual flooding.

Mnemonic for 7 tributaries: "I Started The Long Dangerous Aquatic Trek" → Indravati, Sun Kosi, Tama Kosi, Likhu, Dudh Kosi, Arun, Tamur

★ Kanwar Lake — Appears in Every 3rd BPSC Paper
  • Location: Begusarai district (North Bihar)
  • Type: Oxbow lake (Ganga/Burhi Gandak changed course → cut off meander)
  • Status: Asia's largest freshwater oxbow lake + Ramsar Wetland Site (declared 2020)
  • Ecology: Winter home to migratory birds — Siberian Crane, Bar-headed Goose, Pintail Duck

Section 04

Climate of Bihar

5–6 direct questions per paper — rainfall distribution and seasonal patterns are most tested

Bihar has a humid subtropical climate (Köppen: Cwa type) — hot summers, monsoon rains, cool dry winters. The Tropic of Cancer passing through the south brings significant temperature variation between north and south Bihar.

Three Seasons
SeasonPeriodKey Features for BPSC
Summer (Grishma)March – June35–45°C; Loo (hot dry wind) from W → E; Gaya reaches 45–48°C; drought risk in South Bihar
Monsoon (Varsha)June/July – OctoberSW monsoon from Bay of Bengal branch; NE Bihar gets rain first; 80–90% of annual rain in these months
Winter (Shita)November – February5–15°C; cold waves from NW; January coldest; dense fog in Patna corridor; some rain from western disturbances
Rainfall Distribution
Highest Rainfall Zones
  • Kishanganj — ~1800–2000mm/yr (Bihar's maximum) — Sub-Himalayan + Bay of Bengal proximity
  • Purnia, Araria, Katihar — ~1400–1600mm
  • Champaran, Sitamarhi — ~1200–1400mm
Lowest Rainfall Zones
  • Rohtas, Kaimur, Aurangabad — ~900–1000mm/yr (Bihar's minimum)
  • South Bihar generally drier than North Bihar
  • Gaya — high heat + moderate rain = driest conditions
Climate Numbers — BPSC Tests These Directly
  • Average annual rainfall of Bihar: ~1200 mm
  • Maximum rainfall district: Kishanganj (~1800–2000mm)
  • Minimum rainfall district: Rohtas / Kaimur (~900–1000mm)
  • Monsoon source: Bay of Bengal branch (not Arabian Sea)
  • Monsoon arrival: ~June 10–15 in NE Bihar (Kishanganj first)
  • Highest temperature: Gaya — 45–48°C (May–June)
  • Lowest temperature: West Champaran — 0–3°C (January)
⚠ Classic BPSC Paradox Question

Why does North Bihar flood while South Bihar droughts simultaneously? North Bihar: heavy Himalayan rainfall + river overflow from Nepal → floods. South Bihar: less rainfall + no perennial rivers → drought. Both occur simultaneously in the same season. This Bihar paradox appears regularly in prelims MCQs and mains short answers.

BPSC Soil Quick Facts
  • Most dominant soil: Alluvial soil (~80% of Bihar's area)
  • Makhana grows in: Clayey waterlogged chaur soils — Darbhanga-Madhubani-Sitamarhi belt
  • Laterite found in: Jamui, Banka, Nawada, Munger — southern Bihar (Chota Nagpur fringe)
  • Kankar: Calcium carbonate nodules found in Bhangar soil — hardens the subsoil
  • Problem soils: Acidic laterite (south Bihar, needs lime), Waterlogged clayey (north Bihar chaurs)
